## What a motion language is

Static logos can't carry a brand's energy on screen. A **motion language** is the brand's consistent way of
moving — its **timing, easing, direction, and behaviour**. It's a system, like the colour palette and type:
elements enter, react, and exit in a recognisable way. Done well, motion lives in memory *longer* than static
visuals — we remember a rhythm (the Netflix "N" fold, the Airbnb pulse) more than a still mark.

## The signature move

- Find **one memorable gesture** that expresses the brand and reuse it: a fold, a draw-on, a snap, a specific
  reveal. Build the logo sting and the title entrances around it.
- Derive the gesture from the brand's **shape DNA** — if the mark is built from arcs, things move in arcs; if
  it's sharp and geometric, moves are crisp and linear.
- The signature should read in under a second and survive at any size.

## Consistency across a set

- **One easing curve, one entrance family, one accent timing** across every title/plate/transition in a
  project. That repetition is what the viewer learns and recognises.
- Consistency scales: a single motion system can hold across thousands of deliverables (Google Cloud ran one
  across 2000+) — the discipline is what makes it feel like one brand, not a pile of templates.
- Motion must stay **coherent and intentional** — smooth moves that draw attention to the message, never
  movement that overshadows it. Energy in service of clarity.

## Building it in practice

- Decide the brand's motion rules **once**: easing curve, default entrance direction, stagger rhythm, accent
  timing, the logo reveal. Then reuse them everywhere.
- Store these decisions in the **client's brand folder** (fonts/palette/locked motion DNA), not in a skill —
  see the `motion` skill §3. New locked motion decisions get written back there.
- Optimise the same gesture to work across formats (16:9, 9:16, square) so the brand moves the same on every
  screen.
