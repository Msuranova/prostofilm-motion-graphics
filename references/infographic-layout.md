# Infographic & on-screen layout

## Visual hierarchy

Hierarchy is how you decide what the eye hits first, second, third. Carry it with **size, colour/contrast,
proximity, and typography** — those four do the most work.

- **Three levels, no more.** Primary = the one thing to remember. Secondary = supporting data. Tertiary =
  labels and fine print. A fourth level just muddies the read.
- **Hero is 2–3× the body.** The headline stat or title dwarfs body text; section headers sit between;
  footnotes are smallest. The size jump *is* the hierarchy.
- **One hero per screen.** Two things fighting to be biggest means neither wins. If you have two big numbers,
  give them separate beats.

## Reading patterns & grid

- **F-pattern** for data-dense layouts — viewers scan down the left and across the top. Put key labels and
  values there.
- **Z-pattern** for storytelling layouts — the eye travels top-left → top-right → down → bottom-right. Lay the
  narrative along that path; land the conclusion bottom-right.
- **Rule of thirds.** Divide the frame into a 3×3 grid; place key elements on the lines or at the four
  intersection "power points." The slight asymmetry creates flow and tension; dead-centre reads static and dull.
- **Work on a grid.** Consistent columns, gutters, and margins make alignment automatic and the whole thing
  feel ordered. Misalignment is the fastest way to look amateur.

## White space

- **60/40 rule.** Roughly 40% of the frame should breathe. Empty space is not wasted space — it's what lets the
  hero stand out. Cramming kills the read.
- White space *around* the hero is how you spotlight it without adding any decoration.

## Story structure

- **Three-part arc:** intro (set up the question) → key message (the payoff) → conclusion (the takeaway).
- **One screen = one idea.** Finish a thought, clear it, bring the next. Don't stack unrelated points.
- Map the design to the story before touching visuals: what's the single sentence this frame proves?

## Full-screen animated infographic / explainer

When the graphic owns the whole frame (no footage behind it), the rules tighten:

- **Progressive disclosure is the core move.** Reveal information step by step, never all at once. Each beat
  earns the next ("what comes next?"), which is what holds attention and aids retention.
- **One idea per beat.** Build → land → clear → next. The sequence is the story.
- **Hierarchy through motion.** Use animation to spotlight the key number/trend so the eye lands on it first.
- **Show relationships with movement.** Arrows, morphs, re-flows, connectors — let motion express how data
  points relate, not just that they exist.
- **Stay clean.** Complex animation confuses. Purposeful motion only; decoration dilutes the message.
- **Consistency end to end.** Same colour/font/entrance family the whole way through = professional and legible.
- **Hold each beat to read** ≈ words × 0.3s + 1s, matched to the voiceover line length.

## Fonts in layout

- **Max three typefaces.** Font contrast builds hierarchy; too many fonts read as noise. Usually a display
  face for heroes + one workhorse for body is enough.
- **ALL-CAPS body** gives a uniform letter height — useful when a client wants a tidy block. The accent number
  can be larger; on equal-line layouts keep one size and differentiate with colour/gradient only.
